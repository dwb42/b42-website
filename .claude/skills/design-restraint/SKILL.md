# design-restraint

## Purpose
Ensure a premium feel through restraint and taste. Calm confidence over impressiveness.

## When to use
- Any visual decision: color, motion, effect, ornament, imagery.
- Reviewing work for anything that draws unnecessary attention.
- When tempted to follow a current design trend.
- When a result feels "impressive" — interrogate whether it should.

## Principles
- Avoid trends. Trends date; restraint doesn't.
- Avoid flashiness. Attention-seeking is a tell of weakness.
- Avoid visual tricks. No cleverness for its own sake.
- Calm confidence over impressiveness.
- Taste is subtractive.
- The work should read as inevitable, not designed.

## Instructions
- Prefer neutral, near-monochrome palettes. Black, white, one restrained accent at most.
- Let typography carry the entire experience.
- Remove anything that draws attention away from the words.
- Prefer system defaults over custom chrome unless custom is clearly better and quieter.
- If a choice feels exciting, question it. If it feels quiet and right, keep it.
- Review every addition against: "would removing this hurt the work?" If no, remove it.

## Constraints
- No gradients.
- No animations or transitions beyond imperceptible easing on essential state changes.
- No parallax, scroll-jacking, or scroll-driven effects.
- No shadows, glows, blurs, or filters used as decoration.
- No decorative imagery, illustrations, or iconography.
- No hover effects that exist only to be noticed.
- No dark-mode-as-gimmick; no theme toggles unless requested.
- No loaders, splash screens, or intro sequences.
